1. Introduction

This study focuses on analyzing juvenile Snowshoe Hare data from hare trappings at Bonanza Creek Experimental Forest in Alaska from 1999 to 2012 (Kielland et al. 2017). This analysis focuses on three grid locations within the forest, named Bonanza Black Spruce, Bonanza Mature, and Bonanza Riparian. These sites differ based on the types of trees present, and Bonanza Riparian is located next to a river while the other two are not. This report looks at the change in hare trappings over time, the differences in hare weights based on sex and location, and the relationship between juvenile weight and hind foot length.

2. Data and Analyses

Snowshoe Hare physical data were provided by Dr. Kielland and colleagues, who collected the data at the Bonanza Creek Experimental Forest Long Term Ecological Research (LTER) network site near Fairbanks, Alaska (Kielland et al. 2017). The data contain 3,197 observations from capture-recapture studies of hares at three sites from 1999 to 2012. The observations contain information on variables such as sex (m, f, NA), age (juvenile, adult, and mortality), weight (grams), and hind foot length (mm). This report performs a two-sample t-test, with a significance level (\(\alpha\)) of 0.05, to analyze differences in means. The Cohen’s d effect size is also implemented to determine if the difference in means is significant. A linear regression shows the relationship between snowshoe hare weight and hind foot length.

For each location, the male juvenile snowshoe hares have higher average weights than the female juvenile snowshoe hares. Overall, the males have a higher mean weight than the females. The difference in means is 90.468 grams. From Welch’s two-sample t-test (t(325.02) = -2.71), the p-value is 0.0071. This is very small and indicates that there is only a 0.71% chance that we could have gotten sample means this different if these sample came from populations with the same mean. This does not give evidence to support the null hypothesis that the difference in means is equal to zero. However, the Cohen’s d test shows us that the effect size is small/moderate-leaning (0.29), so the difference in the means between male and females is not very large. The females had a larger sample size than the males, so this must have impacted the t-test. The effect size is less influenced by the sample size.

A linear regression model was used to consider the relationship between juvenile snowshoe hare weight (g) and hind foot length (mm). A significant regression model was found (\(\beta\) = 9.523, F(1,247) = 105.2, p < 0.001) with an R2 of 0.299. The R2 value tells us that 29.9% of variance in weight (g) is predictable by the hind foot length (mm) of the snowshoe hare. The slope of 9.5 indicates that we expect weight to increase by 9.5 g for each 1 mm increase in hind foot length. The y-intercept of -280 does not make sense in this case because that would mean that hind foot length equals zero and it is impossible to have a negative weight. There is a moderate positive correlation between juvenile snowshoe hare weight and hind foot length (r = 0.55, t(247) = 10.26. The Pearson’s r correlation value ranges from -1 to 1, with negative one indicating a strong negative correlation and positive 1 indicating a strong positive correlation (and zero meaning no correlation). The Pearson’s r is positive in this case because the slope is positive, meaning an increase in hind foot length is related to an increase in weight. The Pearson’s r is r = 0.55, so this is a moderate positive correlation. Some assumptions of the linear model are of concern. The residuals are not very evenly spread/constant along the fitted value. Furthermore, the QQ-plot shows a trend away from linearity at smaller values (-3 to -1). There does not appear to be any extreme outliers, however.

Summary

Initial Findings

  • Number of juvenile Snowshoe Hare trappings was lower in recent years, with 1999 having the highest number of trappings. This may be related to the amount of effort put in each year.

  • Juvenile male snowshoe hares weigh more than female snowshoe hares, on average. The differences in sample size may have impacted the t-test because the Cohen’s d test shows us that the effect size is small/moderate-leaning (0.29), meaning the difference in the means between male and females is not very large.

  • Juvenile snowshoe hare weight and hind foot length are moderately positively correlated (r = 0.55) and this relationship was significant.

Next Steps

  • Determine the amount of effort put into trapping hares each year to see if this had an impact on the number of hares caught.

  • Include sex and location in the linear regression analysis.
