The number of juvenile snowshoe hare trappings at Bonanza Creek was much lower in 2012 compared to 1999. The maximum number of trappings was 126 (1999) and the minimum was 2 (2010). The average number of juvenile hares trapped each year was 31.5, while the median was 18.5. The amount of effort put in each year may affect these counts. In future analyses, counts should be standardized by the number of days put into trapping snowshoe hares each year.

Figure 2 shows that male juvenile snowshoe hares weigh more than female juvenile snowshoe hares.The box plots indicate that while the data are fairly spread, the medians are higher for the male weight compared to the female weight. For each location, the male juvenile snowshoe hares have higher average weights than the female juvenile snowshoe hares. Overall, the males have a higher mean weight than the females. The difference in means is 90.468 grams. From the two-sample t-test, the p-value is 0.0071. This is very small and indicates that there is only a 0.71% chance that we could have gotten sample means this different if these sample came from populations with the same mean. This does not give evidence to support the null hypothesis that the difference in means is equal to zero. However, the Cohen’s d test shows us that the effect size is small/moderate-leaning (0.29), so the difference in the means between male and females is not very large. The females had a larger sample size than the males, so this must have impacted the t-test. The effect size is less influenced by the sample size.
